# Fundraising Basics for First-Time Founders

Raising money is not a milestone in itself. It is a tool to buy time and speed for a business that
already shows some evidence of working. Founders who raise too early, without a clear plan for
what the money will prove, often struggle later.

## Common Early-Stage Funding Sources
- **Bootstrapping**: using personal savings or revenue to fund the company, keeping full control
  and ownership.
- **Friends and family rounds**: small, informal early checks, usually before there is much
  traction to show.
- **Angel investors**: individuals investing their own money, often in exchange for equity or a
  convertible note, sometimes bringing mentorship or industry connections.
- **Pre-seed and seed funds**: institutional investors writing early checks in exchange for equity,
  usually expecting a clear founding team and some early signal (waitlist, pilot customers, or a
  working product).

## What Investors Look For Early
1. A founding team that understands the problem deeply and can execute quickly.
2. Evidence that a real problem exists and that the proposed solution addresses it.
3. A large enough market that the business can become significant if it works.
4. Some early signal of momentum, even if small: user growth, revenue, or strong engagement.

## Preparing to Raise
- Keep a simple, honest narrative: the problem, why now, the solution, traction so far, and what
  the round will unlock.
- Know your numbers: burn rate, runway, and what milestone the new funding is meant to reach.
- Avoid raising more than needed just because it is offered. More money can mean less discipline
  and a higher bar to hit at the next round.

## Founder Reminder
A term sheet is the start of a long relationship with an investor, not just a wire transfer.
Reference-check investors the same way they reference-check founders.
