# Go-to-Market Strategy for Early Startups

A go-to-market strategy answers a simple question: how will the first real customers find out
about the product and decide to try it. Early-stage startups usually cannot afford broad marketing
and need a narrow, repeatable channel instead.

## Choosing an Initial Channel
- Pick one primary channel to start (for example: content and SEO, cold outreach, communities and
  forums, or partnerships) rather than spreading thin across many at once.
- Favor channels where the founder can get direct, fast feedback from customers, since early
  go-to-market work doubles as product research.
- Channels that work well for one type of product (viral consumer apps) often do not transfer to
  another (enterprise software), so copying a well-known company's channel blindly is risky.

## Positioning
- Speak in the customer's language about their problem, not in the founder's language about the
  technology behind the solution.
- A clear, narrow positioning statement beats a broad one: naming exactly who the product is for
  and what specific outcome it delivers.

## Early Pricing
- Early pricing is a hypothesis, not a fixed decision. It should be revisited as more is learned
  about willingness to pay.
- Charging too little early can attract the wrong customers and make it harder to raise prices
  later. Many founders under-price out of fear of rejection.

## Signs the Go-to-Market Motion Is Working
- Customers can be acquired somewhat repeatably, not just through one-off founder effort.
- Word of mouth or referrals start appearing without being explicitly requested.
- The cost and effort of acquiring a customer is understood well enough to make a rough forecast.

Go-to-market strategy should evolve as the product and audience become clearer. What works to get
the first ten customers rarely stays the only channel needed to get the first thousand.
