# Hiring the First Team

The first five to ten hires shape a startup's culture far more than any written values document.
Early hiring mistakes are also more costly than later ones, because a small team amplifies both
strengths and weaknesses.

## Who to Hire First
- Generalists who are comfortable with ambiguity tend to outperform narrow specialists in the
  earliest stage, since roles and priorities shift quickly.
- Look for evidence of ownership: people who have shipped something end-to-end before, even
  outside a traditional job (side projects, freelance work, open-source contributions).
- Prioritize people who are genuinely excited about the specific problem the company is solving,
  not just the idea of joining a startup.

## Structuring Early Compensation
- Early employees often trade some cash compensation for equity, reflecting the higher risk and
  potential upside.
- Be transparent about the company's stage, runway, and risk level during hiring conversations.
  Surprises after someone joins damage trust quickly on a small team.
- Vesting schedules protect both the company and the employee by aligning long-term commitment
  with ownership.

## Culture Formation
- Whatever behavior a founder tolerates from the first hires becomes the default culture, whether
  intentional or not.
- Document decisions and reasoning early, even informally. It helps future hires understand why
  things are done a certain way.

## Common Pitfalls
- Hiring too many senior people too early, before there is a stable enough problem for them to
  execute against.
- Delaying the first hire too long out of fear of losing control, which can slow the company down
  at a critical stage.

The goal of early hiring is not to build a full team, it is to add people who make the founder
faster without adding proportional coordination overhead.
