# Validating a Startup Idea

Before writing a single line of code, a founder's first job is to test whether a problem is real
and whether people will pay to have it solved. Idea validation is not about asking friends if they
"like" the idea. It is about finding evidence of pain.

## Signs of a Real Problem
- People are already paying for a clumsy workaround (spreadsheets, manual processes, a competitor
  they complain about).
- The problem shows up repeatedly across many different potential customers, not just one.
- When you describe the problem to a stranger in the target market, they finish your sentence.

## Cheap Ways to Validate
1. **Problem interviews**: Talk to 15-20 potential customers. Ask about their current workflow and
   what frustrates them, without pitching your idea. Let them bring up the pain point unprompted.
2. **Landing page test**: Build a single page describing the solution and measure signups or
   waitlist interest before building anything.
3. **Concierge MVP**: Manually deliver the service by hand (emails, spreadsheets, calls) to a
   handful of early users before automating anything.
4. **Pre-sales**: Ask for a deposit or letter of intent. Willingness to pay before the product
   exists is the strongest signal available.

## Common Mistakes
- Validating the solution instead of the problem. Founders often fall in love with their answer
  and unconsciously steer interviews toward confirming it.
- Talking only to people who are polite and encouraging rather than people who will actually buy.
- Skipping validation because "I already know this is a good idea." Conviction is not evidence.

A good rule of thumb: if you cannot find ten people who already spend time or money fighting this
problem today, the market may be too small or too early for a company.
